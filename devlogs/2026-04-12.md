Key cross-lingual observations from Telugu implementation:
1. **Hindi → Telugu stop transfer:** All Telugu voiceless stops (glottis=0.62)
   and voiced stops (glottis=0.20) share encoding with Hindi stop pairs.
   Stop-place locus transfer from Hindi is strong. However, Telugu lacks the
   aspirated series /pʰ bʱ/ etc. — Hindi decoder may hallucinate aspiration.
2. **Tamil → Telugu retroflex lateral transfer:** /ɭ/ (tongue_b=0.58) has
   identical encoding — near-zero adaptation cost for this segment.
3. **/ɻ/ gating requirement:** Tamil-pretrained decoders that have learned
   /ɻ/ will over-generate it in Telugu contexts. Language-ID gate is critical.
4. **Voiced stop Delta:** Δglottis=0.42 between voiceless (0.62) and voiced (0.20)
   is the primary voicing axis. Same Δ as Hindi — consistent representation.
5. **5-nasal vs 6-nasal:** Telugu's /n/ (tongue_b=0.22) covers the dental-alveolar
   zone without a /n̪/ phoneme. Tamil-pretrained decoders may insert /n̪/ → /n/
   confusions in Telugu contexts (Δtongue_b = 0.10).

### Hypotheses

H1: Hindi-pretrained decoder will achieve <10% stop locus error on Telugu, but
    will generate aspirated outputs at ~30% rate without language-ID gating.
H2: Tamil-pretrained decoder will generate /ɻ/ in ~20-30% of Telugu retroflex
    liquid contexts (/ɭ/ vs /ɻ/ tone_b difference = only 0.04 at the margin).
H3: Telugu's 5-nasal /n/ (tongue_b=0.22) will be confused with Tamil's /n̪/
    (tongue_b=0.12) by a Tamil-pretrained decoder in ~15% of dental contexts.
H4: Vowel quality transfer from Tamil to Telugu is near-perfect (identical encoding);
    vowel length transfer is also near-perfect (same encoding convention).
